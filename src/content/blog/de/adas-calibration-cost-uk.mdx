<h2>What Is ADAS Calibration and When Is It Required?</h2>

<p>ADAS (Advanced Driver Assistance Systems) calibration is the process of realigning the sensors, cameras, and radar units that power safety systems like:</p>
<ul>
<li>Lane Keep Assist / Lane Departure Warning</li>
<li>Emergency Autonomous Braking (AEB)</li>
<li>Adaptive Cruise Control / Forward Collision Warning</li>
<li>Blind Spot Monitoring</li>
<li>Rear Cross Traffic Alert</li>
<li>Surround View / 360° Parking Cameras</li>
</ul>

<p>Calibration is required whenever any of the following work is carried out:</p>
<ul>
<li>Windscreen replacement (camera is usually mounted to the screen)</li>
<li>Front bumper removal or replacement (radar module behind bumper)</li>
<li>Bonnet replacement or major bonnet work</li>
<li>Suspension or wheel alignment work</li>
<li>Any sensor, camera, or module replacement</li>
<li>After any front-end collision damage, even minor</li>
</ul>

<p>In 2026, according to <a href="https://www.thatcham.org" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Thatcham Research</a>, over 91% of new vehicles sold in the UK include at least one ADAS system. This percentage extends backward to most vehicles built from 2018 onwards that remain in common workshop service.</p>

<h2>Static vs Dynamic Calibration: The Key Difference</h2>

<h3>Static Calibration</h3>
<p>Performed in the workshop bay using a physical calibration target (a precisely printed and dimensioned board or reflector) positioned at a specific distance and angle relative to the vehicle on a level surface. The diagnostic tool communicates with the ADAS control module and processes the camera/radar signal against the known-position target to set alignment parameters.</p>

<h3>Dynamic Calibration</h3>
<p>The vehicle must be driven on a suitable road (requirements vary: typically a marked road, good visibility, specific speed range of 30–80 km/h, for a minimum distance) while the ADAS system self-calibrates using real-world lane markings or radar targets. Many camera systems require a combination of static then dynamic calibration for full completion.</p>

<h2>The OEM Data Requirement: Why Generic Procedures Are Dangerous</h2>

<p>This is critical. ADAS calibration specifications — target distance, target height, target lateral offset from vehicle centreline, lighting requirements, maximum acceptable surface inclination — are specific to each vehicle make, model, year, and engine/equipment variant.</p>

<p>A generic calibration procedure that works within broad tolerance for one camera module will be dangerously out of specification for another manufacturer's system. A lane-keep camera that appears to have been calibrated may be set 2–3 degrees off vertical — producing a system that either generates false alerts constantly or, worse, fails to recognise genuine lane departures consistently.</p>

<h2>Step-by-Step: How to Perform a Forward Camera Static Calibration</h2>

<ol>
<li><strong>Position the vehicle</strong> — Park on a level, clean surface in a well-lit workshop bay. Ensure the vehicle is at the specified height (check tyre pressures are at OEM specification).</li>
<li><strong>Access calibration specifications</strong> — Log into <a href="https://www.workshopdata.us/autodata">AutoData</a> or <a href="https://www.workshopdata.us/alldata">AllData</a>. Navigate to the ADAS calibration section for the vehicle make, model, and year.</li>
<li><strong>Set up the target</strong> — Following the OEM specification, position the calibration target at the correct distance (typically 2–6 metres ahead of the vehicle), correct height, and correct lateral alignment from the vehicle centreline. Use laser measuring tools to confirm exact positioning.</li>
<li><strong>Connect the diagnostic tool</strong> — Access the ADAS or camera control module through your all-system scanner.</li>
<li><strong>Initiate the calibration routine</strong> — Follow the diagnostic tool prompts. The camera module will process the target image and set its alignment parameters. The procedure typically takes 5–15 minutes.</li>
<li><strong>Verify calibration complete</strong> — Check that no ADAS-related DTCs are stored after the procedure. Test the system function by test-driving the vehicle (where safe) and confirming ADAS systems are responding correctly.</li>
<li><strong>Document your work</strong> — Record the calibration performed, the platform and procedure reference used, and the test result. This is important for both customer communication and professional liability.</li>
</ol>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

<h3>How much does ADAS calibration cost at a main dealer in the UK?</h3>
<p>Main dealer ADAS calibration typically costs 40–80% more than well-equipped independent workshops. Expect £150–£400 for single-system calibration and £600–£1,500 for a full suite reset at a franchised dealer in the UK.</p>

<h3>Does every windscreen replacement require ADAS calibration?</h3>
<p>Any vehicle with a forward-facing camera mounted to the windscreen requires camera calibration after windscreen replacement. This is the majority of vehicles built after 2018 with any lane-assist or AEB functionality. Glazing firms increasingly require the customer to arrange post-replacement calibration independently.</p>

<h3>Can I perform ADAS calibration without a calibration target board?</h3>
<p>Some vehicles support dynamic-only calibration (road-test based), which can be performed without a physical static target. However, most camera-based systems require static calibration first. Attempting dynamic-only calibration on a system requiring static will typically result in a fault code and incomplete calibration.</p>

<h3>How long does ADAS calibration take?</h3>
<p>Static calibration of a single forward-facing camera typically takes 45–90 minutes including setup. A full ADAS suite with multiple systems takes 3–6 hours. This is why labour is the primary cost component in ADAS calibration billing.</p>

<h3>Which ADAS calibration equipment is the best value in 2026?</h3>
<p>For independent workshops starting out, the Autel MA600 or Autel ADAS Pro provide good universal coverage at moderate cost (£1,500–£4,000). For workshops investing at scale, Bosch DAS 5000 or Hella Gutmann stations provide OEM-level coverage with dedicated manufacturer support.</p>
